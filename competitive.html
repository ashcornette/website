The landscape

three camps solve this problem today — subscription delivery, inventory trackers, and shared lists. none of them learn how fast a household actually burns through things. that's the gap we sit in.

Direct competitors
apps built to track stock and restocking
Restock: Shopping List & Planner closest
tracks purchase cycles with 7 / 14 / 30 / 60-day presets and flags items by urgency — and it's offline-first and private, much like us. the nearest thing on the store, but you set the interval; it never learns your real rate.
Supplies
a manual home-inventory app for food and other daily necessities — track what you have, watch expiry dates, push low items to a shopping list. shares through Apple Reminders. reactive by design: you tell it everything, every time.
Indirect competitors
tools people already reach for instead
Amazon Subscribe & Save
fixed-schedule auto-delivery, 5–15% off for five or more items to one address. closes the whole loop — but on a rigid cadence that ignores how fast you really use things.
AnyList
a shared grocery list and recipe organizer that also does barcode entry, a pantry list, and one-tap reorder through Instacart, Walmart, Kroger and Amazon Fresh. well-shared and feature-rich — but every item is entered by hand, and it never learns timing.
Apple Reminders real default
free and already on every iPhone — auto-categorized, shareable grocery lists. this, plus plain pen-and-paper, is the real default we have to beat, not another paid app. no stock awareness, no prediction.

Positioning map

two axes pull the field apart — whether it learns when to restock (reactive vs predictive), and what it costs you to keep it fed (logging effort). plotted, the picture matches the matrix: the predictive, low-effort corner is empty.

Amazon shares our low-effort height, but ships on a fixed schedule blind to your real pace. Restock planner only edges rightward on cycles you set by hand. the adaptive, low-effort corner stays ours alone.

Where we win

for busy households that keep running out of everyday essentials, ours is the restocking app that warns you before you run out — not after — by learning how fast your household actually uses things, instead of relying on fixed schedules or manual lists.

  • it learns your real pace — you never set an interval or a threshold. none of them do this
  • hands-off and smart at once — Amazon is hands-off but blind; AnyList, Reminders and Supplies are flexible but hand-maintained
  • the timing layer between a rigid subscription and a manual list — that's the open gap
The catch — to stress-test in research
prediction only works if people keep feeding it — the same upkeep Supplies and AnyList quietly rely on, and where most apps lose people past month three. Amazon could also bolt learned timing onto Subscribe & Save in a sprint. and watch the framing: Restock planner is already offline-first and private, so neither "no AI" nor "private and offline" is the wedge — the learned-timing outcome has to carry it.
