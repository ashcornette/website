mvp → mvbp · week 10 · business thinking

the mvp proves the prediction works. the mvbp is the smallest owduv someone actually pays for.
Where the line goes

the full mechanism is free — capture, inference, prediction. paywalling any part of it would starve the free tier of the data that feeds the model and reduce owduv to the manual trackers it exists to replace. nobody would meet the magic before the paywall. instead, the cap is the meter — the free tier runs the whole loop on a limited set of items, and you pay for room, not for the promise to switch on.

The split
free — full loop, metered
  • email auto-capture included
  • depletion inference + notifications
  • reorder links affiliate stays free
  • item cap — number under test
  • single user
owduv plus — scale
  • unlimited items
  • shared household sync
  • multi-inbox · multi-retailer
  • full purchase history
  • $1.99 / mo · $14.99 / yr — hypothesis

two structural calls sit inside this split. affiliate lives on the free side — it scales with click volume, so the users who never subscribe still generate revenue rather than being locked away from the second stream to protect the first. and the cap is itself a hypothesis — households track 15–30 skus, so the cap has to sit under the natural set for the meter to run, but high enough for the prediction to prove itself first. the pilot tests 8 vs 10 vs 12 rather than assuming.

Three objectives, each with a kill threshold
01 · revenue
two instruments, two questions. a fake-door upgrade screen inside the usability prototype validates line placement only — which features people expect to sit behind the pay line. willingness-to-pay gets its own denominator: a priced waitlist page with a card-capture step, because interest is not revenue.
pass — ≥3% of waitlist visitors enter a card at $1.99  ·  kill — interest without cards → reprice or move the meter
02 · deliverability
a wizard-of-oz pilot, ten households — receipts forwarded to a shared inbox, parsed by hand, reflected in the app. this validates the thing that matters — whether corrections feel like logging — without building the parser first, and it is honest about week-12 scope. the same ten households carry the cap test: different caps assigned across them, watching who hits the meter.
pass — ≥80% finish onboarding · ≤1 correction / user / week (the ~95% parse bar the real parser inherits)  ·  kill — corrections feel like logging → the wedge collapses
03 · repeatability
unit economics on low-margin consumables, with affiliate as the floor. the subscription carries the ceiling; affiliate revenue per free active carries the base — both are measured, neither is assumed.
pass — month-2 retention ≥40% · organic cac < $5 against ltv ~$19–36 + affiliate per active  ·  kill — retention <25% → pain too shallow to sustain a subscription
The conversion moment carries the positioning

the most sensitive permission owduv asks for — inbox access — is granted at free signup, not at upgrade. paying never reads as surrendering more data; upgrade copy sells room, not access. on-device parsing is stated plainly at both moments, because the upgrade screen is where the privacy positioning either holds or reads as bait.

Before charging anyone
app store iap — no custom billing at this scale
privacy policy — written before the pilot, not after
single support inbox — <48h response, one person
one-tap cancel — churn data is only honest if leaving is easy
export-your-data — the privacy positioning, made structural
