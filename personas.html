draft — week 7 · proto-personas

personas

proto-personas — assumption-based, built from the value proposition canvas rather than primary research. they name who owduv is for and the friction it removes.

each carries one half of the differentiation: the primary persona is about mental load and adaptive timing; the secondary is about shared-household coordination. the assumptions behind them are flagged to validate — interviews replace these guesses with evidence in the research phase.

primary persona

Jen — the default restocker

34 · full-time · two adults + a toddler · moderate tech comfort

Jen is the one who, by default, notices the hand soap is nearly gone — usually while standing at the sink with wet hands. she keeps a running list in her head, means to write it down, and remembers at the worst possible moment. she's busy, and household upkeep is one more channel of low-grade noise she'd rather mute.

goals

  • keep the house stocked without holding it all in her head
  • stop the last-minute pharmacy runs
  • spend less attention on upkeep

frustrations

  • only notices something's out when she reaches for it
  • a subscription box shipped detergent she didn't need yet while the toothpaste ran dry
  • every list app she's tried fell out of use within a week

currently uses

  • a half-abandoned reminders list
  • memory
  • an Amazon Subscribe & Save she keeps meaning to cancel

needs

  • something that learns her household's real pace
  • a warning before she runs out — not after
  • no obligation to log every use

"i don't want another list to keep up with. i want to just — not run out."

to validate — whether "less mental load" lands harder than "save money," and whether she'll trust timing she didn't set herself or needs to see why a prompt fired.

secondary persona

Jason — the shared-house coordinator

27 · shares a flat with two others · high tech comfort · splits a supplies kitty

Jason lives with two roommates and a shared shelf of things nobody owns. paper towels run out and everyone assumes someone else clocked it. Devin usually ends up being that someone — or buying a third bottle of dish soap because no one knew the other two existed.

goals

  • coordinate restocking across the household
  • kill the duplicate buys
  • keep the load fair without playing manager

frustrations

  • no shared view — everyone works off a different mental list
  • duplicate purchases quietly drain the kitty
  • reminders posted to the group chat scroll away by evening

currently uses

  • a pinned note in the group chat
  • AnyList — tried once, abandoned (only works if everyone updates it)
  • the occasional all-caps "we're out of TP" text

needs

  • one shared view the whole house sees
  • a list that stays current on its own
  • no dependency on everyone remembering to log

"we don't need a chore chart. we need to all be looking at the same shelf."

to validate — whether shared visibility alone settles the fairness friction, or whether people still want explicit "you're up" assignment.
