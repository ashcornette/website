Prototype 01 — cold start
The cold start

a restocking app knows nothing on the day it's installed. it can't predict a run-out until it knows two things — what you buy, and how fast you get through it. every tracker on the shelf solves this by asking you to type your cupboard in first. that's the wall. that's where people quit, and it's why the category never sticks.

so the first run doesn't ask for a list. it asks for a receipt. this prototype is the whole of that first run — install to a working restock list, without logging anything.

The flow
  1. 01
    first run three doors, no list — an inbox, a retailer, or a single receipt. skip is still there, but skip means typing, and the whole point is that nobody should have to.
  2. 02
    connect a source email reads receipts and nothing else. retailers read order history. the scan is the low-commitment door — one photo, no account, for people who won't hand over an inbox to an app they installed four minutes ago.
  3. 03
    reading the parse runs on the phone. this screen isn't a loading spinner buying time — it's there to make on-device processing legible at the exact moment trust is being asked for.
  4. 04
    found eight things you restock, three already low. the payoff has to land before any effort is requested — this is the screen the whole cold start is built to reach.
  5. 05
    stock view the cold start ends where the normal app begins — and where the reorder prototype below picks up.

the privacy line is load-bearing, not decoration. asking for inbox access is the largest thing owduv ever asks for, and it asks in the first thirty seconds — so "processed on your device — raw purchases never leave your phone" sits on the screen making the request, not buried in a settings page.

What the cold start is testing
one question, really: will someone connect an inbox to an app they've had for thirty seconds? the flow is built on the bet that a visible payoff — eight items, three already low — is worth more than the guarantee. if people take the scan door instead, or skip to manual, the bet is wrong and the cold start has to earn trust before it asks. the second thing to watch is the found screen: if eight items reads as thin or as wrong, the parse isn't the problem, the framing is.
Prototype 02 — reorder
Adding a reorder

the cold start earns the list. the reorder is where the list pays. it's the only path in the app that touches money — the affiliate cut is taken at the moment someone reorders — so every decision on these four screens is a revenue decision wearing a UX costume.

the alert was never the hard part. the hard part is the twenty seconds after it. owduv's answer is to pre-fill everything it already knows and then hand the cart over — it fills, it doesn't sell, and it never takes payment.

The flow
  1. 01
    stock view two running low, one out. sorted by urgency, not alphabet — the only reason anyone opens this app is the top of the list, so the top of the list is the whole screen.
  2. 02
    item detail runs out in about four days, around apr 18. the prediction shows its work — cadence, last buy, usual quantity, and five purchases plotted with the predicted next as a dashed mark. a guess you can't inspect is a guess you won't act on.
  3. 03
    reorder sheet quantity pre-filled to the two packs you usually buy, retailer pre-set to the one you saved. if the defaults are right it's two taps — and the defaults are only right because the cold start did its job.
  4. 04
    hand-off continue to retailer. owduv fills the cart and stops — checkout happens in the retailer's app, and a toast is the only thing that comes back.

snooze sits under the button on purpose. a nudge that can only be obeyed or ignored teaches people to ignore it — snooze is how the model hears "you guessed early" without anyone having to say it.

What the reorder is testing
the hand-off is the bet and the leak in the same move. owduv gives checkout away to a retailer it doesn't control, and every app switch costs completion — so the number that matters isn't taps to reorder, it's what share of continue-to-retailer taps end in an actual purchase. that share is the revenue line from the business model canvas, tested for the first time. worth watching too: whether "reorder · 2 packs" reads as convenient or as presumptuous. the defaults carry this flow, and a confidently wrong default costs more trust than no default at all.
Prototype 03 — shared household
The share feature

a household is the unit, not a person. two people restocking from the same cupboard is how you end up with four bottles of dish soap and no toilet paper — which is the exact failure owduv exists to prevent, reintroduced by owduv itself. an app that nudges every member independently doesn't solve double-buying. it automates it.

so the shared view isn't a growth feature bolted on the side. it's the thing that stops the product from causing the problem it sells against. sam reordered dish soap two days ago — that has to be visible before you reorder it too.

Try it
open in figma →

this one is a single screen, not a walk — it's reached from the shared tab in the bottom nav, so it has no entry flow of its own. three members, a recent feed, and the stock list everyone shares.

What it carries
  1. 01
    members you, sam, alex — three avatars and an invite. the household is small by design; this is a cupboard, not an org chart.
  2. 02
    recent sam reordered dish soap · 2d. alex added sponges · 4d. you marked paper towels low · 5d. it's a feed, not a notification — a household doesn't need to be told, it needs to be able to check.
  3. 03
    shared stock one list, same statuses as your own view. the reorder above runs from here identically — sharing is a layer over that flow, not a second one.

the feed only earns its place if it lands before the reorder, not after. seeing "sam reordered dish soap" the day after you bought it is a receipt for a mistake — which is an argument that this belongs on the reorder sheet too, not only on its own tab.

What the share feature is testing
the bet is that visibility is enough — that "sam reordered dish soap · 2d" prevents the double buy on its own, without owduv locking the item or making anyone ask permission. if households still double-buy, the feed is too passive and the warning has to move into the reorder sheet where the money is. the harder question is whether an activity feed reads as useful or as surveillance: "you marked paper towels low" is a record of your behaviour shown to people you live with, and that lands very differently in a family than in a share house. worth asking who a household actually wants to see what.
